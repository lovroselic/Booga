Help:

Charge each jump carefully and try to land where you intended. Failing that, somewhere non-fatal is also acceptable.

Finish the level within the allotted time to earn a time bonus, because mere survival apparently deserves no applause.

Avoid deadly creatures at all costs. They are not interested in negotiating, and you are conveniently bite-sized.

KEYS:

LEFT ... hold to charge a jump to the left; release to discover whether it was enough

RIGHT ... hold to charge a jump to the right; optimism remains optional

CTRL + CURSOR KEYS ... look around before leaping into something regrettable

F4 ... pause/resume the game, and briefly reconsider your decisions

About:

Bugaboo (The Flea), created by Spanish programmer Pedro Ruiz, was one of the most infuriating games ever devised and naturally, one of the first games I played on my Commodore 64. Originally released for the ZX Spectrum, it delighted players with repetitive music, panic-driven movement and the constant suspicion that the flea had developed a personal grudge against them.

I attempted to complete its first level countless times, never realising that it was also the last level and that somebody who actually knew what they were doing could finish the entire game in about half a minute. My own attempts generally lasted longer, although considerably less happened. Except feeding the dragon with own body.

Therefore, when I accepted the challenge of creating an adaptation, my first design decision was obvious: multiple levels. There seemed little point in building an entire engine merely to reproduce thirty seconds of gameplay and several hours of emotional damage.

My main development goal was once again to escape the grid constraints imposed by my own ENGINE, by using the grid in a slightly less obedient manner. A careful observer may notice that the irregular cave formations are assembled from reusable patterns, rotated, flipped and combined into more organic shapes. A background mask then provides the collision data required for sprite-to-cave interaction, allowing the scenery to look irregular while the engine quietly continues counting squares underneath.
